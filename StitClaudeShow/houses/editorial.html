House 01 · of Eight

Editorial.

Magazine typography applied to software. Oversized serifs, pull quotes, generous leading. Headlines that earn their scale.

By Stit'Claude Editorial · April MMXXVI

Typography is the detail that no one notices until it's wrong — and then everyone does. This is the oldest law of editorial design, written into every style manual since Vignelli, and ignored by most software products the moment they decide that "a slight indigo gradient" will solve their heading problem.

Editorial as a house is not a style. It is a deference — a willingness to treat words the way magazines have treated them since Esquire in 1962. Display type that earns its size. Body type that respects the reader's eye. Marginalia that whispers rather than shouts. A pull quote that feels authored, not templated.

What the house refuses

All text set at 14 to 16 pixels because "it's more readable." Sixteen to twenty-two for body serif is what readability actually looks like. Gray boxes around pull quotes. Drop caps that use the wrong font. Byline stacks that read like metadata.

Centered body copy. Full-width text. Justified text without hyphenation. Any caption that sounds like a form field. These are not matters of taste. They are matters of how the eye moves across a page, and the eye has not changed since Gutenberg.

What the house allows

A headline that breaks out of its container — this is editorial signal, not a bug. Body italic serif at 1.6 line-height minimum. Drop caps on the first paragraph of long-form, one per page. Section marks (§) used sparingly, for navigation, never for decoration. And negative space, always, always more than you think.

№ 1 · marginalia Before the web, marginalia were the scribbled annotations readers added in the margins of books — glosses, objections, corrections. Editorial design reintroduces them as a deliberate column.
№ 2 · drop cap A large initial capital at the start of a chapter or long paragraph, descended from illuminated manuscripts. On screen, float: left + ::first-letter pseudo — five to seven lines deep is correct.
№ 3 · leading The vertical space between baselines. Magazine body sits at 1.6 to 1.75. Most software ships at 1.4 by default and wonders why it feels cramped.
№ 4 · pull quote A sentence extracted from body copy, repeated at display size to draw the eye. It is never the most important sentence. It is the one that makes you want to read the ones around it.
№ 5 · kicker The small line above a headline — here, "House 01 · of Eight" — that situates the article within a larger publication. Derived from newspaper tradition.

Plate II

The Canon.

Where Editorial lives, outside the software industry. Study these when you've lost the thread of what this house means.

1962–1978
Esquire magazine

George Lois era. Covers that were editorial arguments — typography used as rhetoric, not decoration. Still the reference for display type with a point of view.

2015–present
Aeon magazine

Long-form essays rendered with serif discipline. Proof that the web can do what print does, given restraint. Generous leading, quiet palette, author voice.

1994–present
Wired magazine

Early issues pushed magazine type into tech writing. Still a reference for scale and hierarchy. The fluorescent era (1993–98) remains unmatched.

2019–present
Are.na editorial

A calm, considered editorial site that feels like a magazine that survived the blog era. Typography-first, image-light, never hurried.
