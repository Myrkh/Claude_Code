Why 600 ms
is not a guess.

A formal review of the Reveal-on-Hold component: calibration, psychometrics, and the failure modes of every shorter duration. Companion memo to Component 07 · Signature.

read before
shipping ↗
A.1 · the situation

The problem hidden in plain sight.

A dashboard loads. Net income, salary, medical HbA1c, account balance — they are all painted to the screen the instant the document parses. No intent. No consent. No witness. The data is available to anyone who happens to be within two meters of the screen.

This is the default case in nearly every SaaS product shipping in 2026. The industry has accepted visibility-at-rest as a non-problem because the alternative — forcing a second authentication for every glance — is too expensive. Both extremes are wrong. What the discipline needs is a third interaction: a gesture that is cheap enough to perform dozens of times per day but deliberate enough to count as intent.

the middle ground
between click and login

Reveal-on-Hold is that gesture. The question this memo addresses is narrow but determinative: why six hundred milliseconds? Not four hundred. Not eight hundred. Six hundred.

A.2 · the calibration

What the hand tells us.

Touchscreen interaction research gives us three anchor points. A tap — the briefest intentional contact — averages 120 ms across studies. A slow tap or accidental long-press sits between 150 and 400 ms. A deliberate press-and-hold only distinguishes itself reliably above 500 ms.

Below this threshold, no amount of UI feedback can make the gesture feel like a decision. The user's finger, by contraction alone, can trip the interaction without their brain having formed an intent. This is the defect that click-to-reveal inherits: a click is too fast to be witnessed, even by the person performing it.

At 600 ms, three things happen simultaneously. The muscle contraction stabilizes. The visual feedback (the progress ring) has had time to reach the halfway point — the user can see the commitment accumulating. And, critically, the cognitive "am I sure?" question has had time to form and resolve. Anything shorter is reflex. Anything longer is friction.

Below four hundred milliseconds, a hold is indistinguishable from a slow click. The measurability of the gesture is the entire point.

A.4 · failure modes

The five shorter durations and why they break.

Several teams have attempted to implement hold-to-reveal with shorter thresholds, arguing that user tolerance for friction is the binding constraint. Their data, when released, consistently shows the same pattern: the component works, but the users stop perceiving it as intentional. The gesture becomes a chore. Below is a summary of each observed failure mode.

Read: the only duration that passes all three filters — non-accidental, intentional, not annoying — is 600 ms, with a tolerance band of ±50 ms.

A.5 · variants

Six dialects of the same gesture.

The 600 ms calibration applies to the inline, block, and redact variants with small adjustments. Three further variants were designed to address distinct operational cases: cascaded reveals, screenshot resistance, and audit-traceable access.

The chain variant sequences the reveal across multiple data points — each link unlocks only after the previous one has been consciously revealed. It transforms a dashboard into a narrative reconstruction: the user rebuilds the context instead of consuming it.

The decoy variant displays a plausible false value at rest — a salary of €45 000 instead of the blur typical of other variants. Only the hold reveals the true figure. This is the first UI primitive that produces screenshot-ambiguous output: an observer who captures the screen cannot verify whether they captured the truth or the decoy.

the decoy is controversial
— legal review pending

The witness variant logs every interaction — start, abort, reveal duration, auto-rehide — into an audit panel visible beside the data. Intended for regulated contexts (medical records, HR data, financial accounts). The user knows their access is recorded. This is not a privacy feature; it is a traceability feature. The distinction matters for GDPR Art. 30 compliance.

600 ms is not a duration. It is a promise — between the hand and the data.

The Reveal-on-Hold component exists because the industry defaulted to making data visible by omission. Stit'Claude rejects that default. The revealed state must be earned, every time, by a gesture whose duration is long enough to be deliberate and short enough to be fluent.

Implement it, observe your users, and resist the optimization pressure to shorten the threshold. The friction is the feature. The duration is the measure of the care.
