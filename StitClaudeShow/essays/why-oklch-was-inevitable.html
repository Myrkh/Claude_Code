Color Theory · Perceptual Uniformity

Why OKLCH was inevitable.

Abstract

The RGB color space has governed the web since 1996. It is hostile to both designers and screens — two colors with the same numeric distance from one another can appear wildly different to the eye[1]. OKLCH, by contrast, is built on perceptual models refined over forty years[2]. This experiment demonstrates, with live controls, the four situations in which RGB visibly fails and OKLCH visibly succeeds — and argues that the CSS Color 4 specification[3] makes further use of RGB for design tokens indefensible.

01.

The muddy middle. A two-stop gradient reveals the first failure.

Interpolate between rgb(30,30,220) and rgb(220,220,30) linearly in RGB. The midpoint is rgb(125,125,125) — a desaturated grey. The eye reads this as a dead zone, a muddy band cutting the gradient in half. RGB has no concept of perceived chroma; it is a cube of three voltage values.

Interpolate the equivalent in OKLCH. The midpoint preserves chroma. The gradient reads as a continuous hue shift, never losing saturation. This is not tasteful — it is measurable. The OKLCH model uses a lightness axis (L) that corresponds to human perception, and a chroma axis (C) that remains stable during hue interpolation[4].

Observation: The RGB gradient loses saturation at the midpoint; the OKLCH gradient does not. The difference is not aesthetic — it is perceptual. Any designer who has tried to make a two-color brand gradient in CSS has fought this exact phenomenon.

02.

Equal steps, unequal brightness. Ten hues at the same HSL lightness, compared.

In HSL, lightness: 50% produces colors of wildly different perceived brightness. A yellow at hsl(60 100% 50%) is luminous; a blue at hsl(240 100% 50%) is nearly black. The L axis in HSL is mathematical, not perceptual.

The same ten hues expressed in OKLCH at L = 0.70 produce colors of visually equal lightness. This is what makes OKLCH indispensable for data visualization, accessibility audits, and any UI system where predictable contrast is a requirement.

Observation: In HSL, yellow dominates and blue disappears. In OKLCH, every hue reads at the same visual weight. This is the OKLCH promise: predictable brightness regardless of hue.

Live comparison. Move the sliders. Watch what breaks.

Below: the same three inputs — lightness, chroma, hue — applied to an HSL approximation and an OKLCH specification. Sweep the hue from 0° to 360° and observe: OKLCH maintains a steady apparent brightness; HSL lurches from dim to brilliant and back.

04.

Generate a ramp. A UI that would have required two designers now needs one line.

A six-step neutral ramp — bg, surface, card, border, muted, fg — used to demand hand-tuning per theme. In OKLCH, the L values alone determine the ramp. Fix the hue. Fix the chroma. Step the lightness. Ship.

Observation: Any hue, any chroma, produces a systematically ordered ramp. The same technique applied to HSL requires hand-tuning every step. This is why design systems shipping in 2026 use OKLCH natively.

OKLCH is not an upgrade. It is the correction of a mistake that lasted thirty years.

RGB was designed for CRT phosphors in the 1960s. HSL was designed for Color Picker UIs in 1978. Both are device models — they describe what a screen does, not what an eye sees.

OKLCH is a perception model. It describes what the viewer experiences, and leaves the device to figure out how to produce it. Every new design system, every accessibility audit, every token library built after 2022 uses OKLCH or should[5].

The inevitability is this: software measured by how it feels to use is software whose foundations must match human perception. RGB did not match. OKLCH does.
