The Anatomy of a Stitch.

On the two-pixel indicator that changed my mind about software.

Stit'Claude Field Notes  ·  by the studio

I.

The detail no one mentions

There is a small, almost invisible design choice in Linear — the project management tool — that took me nearly a year to notice, and then once I noticed it, I could not look at another product the same way. It is a two-pixel coral bar, rounded, glowing faintly, floating at the left edge of the currently-active item in the sidebar. That's the entire feature. That's the whole essay.¹

NOTE  ·  I The indicator — when you see it, you can't unsee it. It is small in the way haiku is small.

Most software marks active items with a filled background: the entire row turns gray, or blue, or purple. This is the default solution. It is the one every tutorial teaches. It is the one every component library ships. And it is, in every single case I have examined carefully, the wrong solution. Because a filled background asks the user to accept that the item has changed. A two-pixel bar does not make any such demand. The item is still the item. Only its context has shifted.

II.

Why the bar works, and the fill doesn't

A filled background is violent. It rewrites the pixels occupied by the label. The text that was black on cream is now black on gray, or white on blue, and the user's eye must re-acquire it. The act of selecting becomes an act of visual disruption. A two-pixel bar, meanwhile, is additive. It is a small mark placed beside the label, not on top of it. The label is unchanged. The user's attention is invited, not commanded.

Dieter Rams said good design is as little design as possible. He was explaining furniture, but the principle translates directly. A filled background is a lot of design — it recolors a rectangle, it redraws text, it introduces a new surface. A two-pixel bar is almost no design: a thin line, one color, one position. It does exactly the job of indicating active state, and nothing else.²

NOTE  ·  II Rams's tenth principle is often quoted as "less, but better". A more accurate translation of weniger, aber besser is something closer to less, except better — the but is adversarial. Less is not a sacrifice; it is the condition of better.

III.

The glow is not decoration

Look closely and you'll see that the bar has a soft coral glow. This is the detail most copycat implementations get wrong. They add the bar but forget the glow, and the result reads as a border — a division between items — rather than as an indicator. The glow is what tells the eye that this is not a structural line. It is a mark, a stamp, a small flag planted in the margin.

In CSS, the glow is a box-shadow with a blur radius of six or eight pixels, in the same hue as the bar but at approximately seventy percent opacity. That single declaration separates a functional line from a communicative mark. It is, in a literal sense, the difference between the bar being seen and the bar being understood.

NOTE  ·  III box-shadow: 0 0 6px color-mix(in oklch, var(--accent) 70%, transparent); The whole idea, in one declaration.

IV.

What this tells us about software

We are told that good software is the sum of its features — that what matters is what the tool does. I think the better software is the sum of its decisions. The features are requested. The decisions are chosen. And a decision like "we will indicate the active item with an additive mark, not a subtractive rewrite" is the kind of choice that, once made, propagates through every subsequent screen. It is a philosophy made visible.

Most products do not make this decision. They ship the filled background, because filled backgrounds are the default of every CSS framework and every design system template in existence. The filled background is not chosen. It is inherited. And software that inherits rather than chooses accumulates these small thoughtlessnesses until the whole product feels, to use the technical term, off.

V.

What to do with this

When you next open your design tool, look at your active-state indicator. Ask yourself whether it was chosen or inherited. If it was inherited, try the bar. Two pixels, rounded, accent color, glow at the same hue. Ship that version. Watch how the rest of the interface reshuffles itself around a more honest decision. The buttons might shrink. The colors might calm. The whole system starts conducting itself with a little more dignity.

This is how one detail changes a product. Not by being impressive — a two-pixel bar is the opposite of impressive — but by being correct. Small correctness, compounded across a thousand decisions, is the difference between a piece of software that feels lived-in and one that feels assembled. The stitch is small. But the garment holds.³
